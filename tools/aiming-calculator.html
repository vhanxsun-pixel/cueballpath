What is Ghost Ball Aiming?

The ghost ball method is the most widely taught aiming system in billiards. The concept is straightforward: you imagine a "ghost" cue ball sitting in the exact position where it needs to contact the object ball to send it into the pocket. Your job is simply to shoot your real cue ball to the center of that imaginary ghost ball position.

This works because when two billiard balls of equal size collide, the object ball travels along the line connecting both ball centers at the moment of impact. By visualizing the ghost ball touching the object ball on the pocket-side, you establish the correct contact line without needing to calculate angles in your head.

Most beginners struggle with ghost ball aiming because they aim at the edge of the object ball rather than at the center of the ghost ball position. The distinction is critical. You are not aiming at a point on the object ball. You are aiming your cue ball's center to arrive at a specific point in space, about one ball-width away from the object ball along the pocket line.

How Cut Angles Work

A cut angle is the angle between the line from the object ball to the pocket and the line from the cue ball to the object ball at contact. A straight-in shot has a zero-degree cut angle. A shot where you barely clip the edge of the object ball might be a 70-degree cut or higher.

Understanding cut angles matters because each angle requires a different aim point on the ghost ball. At zero degrees, you aim directly behind the object ball. As the cut angle increases, your aim point moves further to the side. At 30 degrees, you are aiming roughly at the edge of the object ball. Beyond 45 degrees, shots become progressively thinner and more difficult to execute consistently.

The relationship between cut angle and aim point is not linear, which is why players struggle to estimate by feel alone. A 15-degree cut looks almost straight, but a 45-degree cut demands that you aim at what appears to be empty space beside the object ball. This visual disconnect is where most missed shots come from.

Our calculator displays the exact cut angle for any shot you set up, helping you connect the visual picture on the table to the underlying geometry. Over time, you internalize these angles and no longer need to calculate. You simply recognize the shot shape and know where to aim.

Tips for Better Aim

Consistent aiming starts with a consistent pre-shot routine. Before you get down on the shot, stand directly behind the cue ball looking along the aim line. Find your ghost ball position from this perspective. Only then step into your stance. Rushing this step is the single biggest accuracy killer for intermediate players.

Keep your eyes on the object ball's contact point as you deliver the stroke. Many players look back at the cue ball at the last moment, which shifts their alignment. Trust your aim and watch the object ball. Your body follows your eyes, and looking at the target keeps your delivery on line.

For thin cuts, use reference points on the table. The diamonds along the rail, spots on the cloth, or even tiny imperfections can serve as landmarks for your aim line. External references are more reliable than trying to hold an imaginary point in space, especially under pressure.

Practice with purpose. Rather than shooting random balls, set up the same shot ten times in a row. Adjust your aim point by fractions until you pocket it consistently, then move to a new angle. This deliberate repetition builds the neural pathways that produce automatic, accurate aim in real games.

Finally, accept that aiming systems are training wheels. The ghost ball method, fractional aiming, CTE, and other systems all converge on the same physics. They are different ways to find the correct contact point. Use whichever system resonates with you now, and trust that repeated correct execution will eventually become instinctive.
