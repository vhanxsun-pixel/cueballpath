Pool Cut Angle & Ball Thickness Chart: Complete Reference

A practical lookup table converting cut angles to ball thickness, overlap fractions, and d-values. Covers every angle from 0° to 90° that matters in Chinese 8-ball and American pool.

Every shot in pool that is not perfectly straight involves a cut angle. That angle determines how much of the cue ball overlaps the object ball at the moment of contact, which players refer to as thickness. Understanding the relationship between angle and thickness lets you calibrate your aim faster and recognize shots by their geometry rather than guessing.

This chart gives you the full mapping. Memorize the key reference points, and you will never face a shot where you cannot at least identify the approximate thickness needed.

Understanding the Numbers

Overlap Fraction

Overlap is the percentage of the cue ball diameter that covers the object ball at impact. A full ball hit means 100% overlap. A half ball hit means the cue ball center aligns with the edge of the object ball, producing exactly 50% overlap. The mathematical relationship is:

overlap = 1 - sin(cut_angle)

This formula explains why the relationship is not linear. Going from 0° to 30° loses half your overlap, but going from 30° to 60° only loses another 37%. Thin cuts are disproportionately sensitive to small angle changes.

The d-Value

The d-value is the distance between the centers of the cue ball and object ball projected along the aim line, measured in ball radii. It represents how far off-center the contact is. The formula is:

d = 2R × cos(cut_angle)

When d = 2R (full ball), the centers are aligned. When d = 0, you miss entirely. The d-value is useful because it gives you a physical measurement you can visualize on the table, rather than an abstract percentage.

Thickness Names

Traditional thickness fractions (full, 3/4, 1/2, 1/4) are approximations that have been used by players for over a century. They do not divide the angle range evenly. A half ball is at 30°, not 45°. A quarter ball is at 45°, not at 22.5°. This unevenness is because thickness refers to overlap fraction, which follows a sine curve, not a linear scale.

The Four Angles You Must Memorize

You do not need to memorize the entire table. Four reference points cover most game situations:

  1. 0° — Full ball. Straight shot. Aim directly at the object ball center. The cue ball stops on a stun stroke.
  2. 14° — Three-quarter ball. Looks almost straight but is not. This is where most beginners miss because they treat it as a straight shot. You need to aim slightly off-center.
  3. 30° — Half ball. The most common game angle. The cue ball center aims at the edge of the object ball. After contact, the cue ball deflects at 90° on a stun shot.
  4. 45° — Quarter ball. A thin cut where you barely clip the ball. The object ball barely moves, and the cue ball continues mostly in its original direction.

Once you can instantly recognize these four reference angles, you can interpolate. A shot that looks slightly thinner than a half ball is probably around 35°. Slightly thicker than a quarter ball is around 40°. This mental calibration is faster and more reliable than trying to visualize exact ghost ball positions on every shot.

How Cut Angle Affects Cue Ball Deflection

On a stun shot (no top or bottom spin at impact), the cue ball always deflects 90° from the aim line after contacting the object ball. This is a fundamental physics result for equal-mass elastic collisions.

But most shots are not pure stun shots. Top spin (follow) reduces the deflection angle below 90°, keeping the cue ball moving more forward after contact. Draw (backspin) increases the deflection angle above 90°, pulling the cue ball backward. The exact angles depend on both the cut angle and the amount of spin. For a detailed breakdown of how spin changes the separation angle, see our cue ball control guide.

The key insight: cut angle determines where the object ball goes. Spin determines where the cue ball goes. They are independent decisions you make on every shot.

Why Thin Cuts Are Harder

Look at the overlap column in the table. At 60°, you only have 13% overlap. At 70°, just 6%. This means a 1° aiming error at 70° has a much bigger effect on the contact than a 1° error at 15°. The ball-to-ball contact patch is smaller, so there is less room for error.

Additionally, thin cuts transfer less energy to the object ball. The object ball moves slowly while the cue ball retains most of its speed. This makes distance control unpredictable and explains why professional players avoid extreme thin cuts when possible, choosing instead to play safe or find a different angle.

In practice, most players can consistently make cuts up to about 55-60°. Beyond that, the margin for error becomes so small that even slight imperfections in the stroke or english application cause misses. If you are facing a shot steeper than 60°, consider whether a safety play or a kick shot gives you a higher expected outcome.

Training Your Angle Recognition

The fastest way to build angle recognition is structured repetition. Set up a shot at a known angle, pocket the ball several times, then change the angle. Over time, your brain builds a visual library that matches what you see on the table to the thickness you need.

Our interactive training tool includes an angle training module that generates random shot setups and asks you to identify the cut angle range. It provides instant feedback and tracks your accuracy over time. This gamified approach builds recognition speed much faster than unstructured practice because it forces active decision-making on every shot rather than letting you fall into repetitive patterns.

A practical drill you can do on a real table: place the object ball on the foot spot and position the cue ball at different angles. Before shooting, call out the thickness (half ball, quarter ball, etc.) and then verify by checking whether the cue ball deflects at the expected angle after contact. If your prediction and the result match consistently, your angle recognition is solid.

Applying the Chart in Game Situations

During a game, you do not have time to calculate sine values. Instead, use this mental shorthand:

  • If the shot looks almost straight, it is 10-15°. Aim slightly off center and commit.
  • If the cue ball needs to go to the edge of the object ball, it is around 30°. Classic half ball.
  • If you need to barely clip the object ball, it is 45°+. Consider your options carefully.
  • If the angle looks steeper than 60°, think twice. Safety might be the smart play.

The chart is a reference tool for practice sessions and post-game analysis. In the game itself, your trained visual instinct takes over. The chart helps you build that instinct faster by giving you concrete numbers to calibrate against.
